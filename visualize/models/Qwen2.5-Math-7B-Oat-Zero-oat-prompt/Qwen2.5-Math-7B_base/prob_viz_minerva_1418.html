The marginal rate of substitution (MRS) is the rate at which a consumer is willing to give up one good in exchange for another while maintaining the same level of utility. Mathematically, it is given by the negative ratio of the marginal utilities of the two goods. The utility function is given by: \[ u(x, y) = x^{\frac{1}{2}} + y \] The marginal utility of \(x\) (MUx) is the partial derivative of the utility function with respect to \(x\): \[ MU_x = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{1}{2} x^{-\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} \] The marginal utility of \(y\) (MUy) is the partial derivative of the utility function with respect to \(y\): \[ MU_y = \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = 1 \] The MRS is given by: \[ MRS = -\frac{MU_x}{MU_y} = -\frac{\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}}{1} = -\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} \] At the point \((X, Y)\), the MRS is: \[ MRS = -\frac{1}{2\sqrt{X}} \] </think> <answer> The marginal rate of substitution of the consumer at an arbitrary point \((X, Y)\), where \(X > 0\) and \(Y > 0\), is \(\boxed{-\frac{1}{2\sqrt{X}}}\). </answer>

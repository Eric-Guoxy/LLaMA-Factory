Let's denote the current mean of Maureen's quiz scores by \( M \) and the number of quizzes she has taken so far by \( n \). The total sum of her quiz scores so far is then \( nM \). If Maureen scores an 11 on the next quiz, her new mean will be: \[ \frac{nM + 11}{n+1} = M + 1 \] Multiplying both sides by \( n+1 \) gives: \[ nM + 11 = (n+1)(M + 1) = nM + M + n + 1 \] Simplifying, we get: \[ 11 = M + n + 1 \] \[ M + n = 10 \quad \text{(Equation 1)} \] If Maureen scores an 11 on each of the next three quizzes, her new mean will be: \[ \frac{nM + 11 + 11 + 11}{n+3} = M + 2 \] Simplifying the left side, we get: \[ \frac{nM + 33}{n+3} = M + 2 \] Multiplying both sides by \( n+3 \) gives: \[ nM + 33 = (n+3)(M + 2) = nM + 2n + 3M + 6 \] Simplifying, we get: \[ 33 = 2n + 3M + 6 \] \[ 2n + 3M = 27 \quad \text{(Equation 2)} \] Now we have a system of linear equations: 1. \( M + n = 10 \) 2. \( 2n + 3M = 27 \) We can solve this system by substitution or elimination. Let's use substitution. From Equation 1, we can express \( n \) in terms of \( M \): \[ n = 10 - M \] Substitute this into Equation 2: \[ 2(10 - M) + 3M = 27 \] \[ 20 - 2M + 3M = 27 \] \[ 20 + M = 27 \] \[ M = 7 \] So the current mean of Maureen's quiz scores is \( \boxed{7} \).

To solve this problem, we can use Fick's second law of diffusion. The concentration \(C(x,t)\) of helium at a depth \(x\) and time \(t\) can be described by the following partial differential equation: \[ \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x^2} \] where \(D\) is the diffusion coefficient. The initial condition is that the concentration of helium is constant throughout the glass, so \(C(x,0) = C_0\). The boundary condition is that at \(t = 0\), the concentration at the surface of the glass (i.e., \(x = 0\)) is \(C_0\), and as \(x \to \infty\), the concentration approaches zero. The solution to this equation for the concentration at a depth \(x\) and time \(t\) is given by the error function: \[ C(x,t) = C_0 \left[1 - \text{erf}\left(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{Dt}}\right)\right] \] We need to find the depth \(x\) at which the concentration has decreased to \(1/3\) of its initial value after 10 minutes (600 seconds). So, we set \(C(x,600) = \frac{C_0}{3}\): \[ \frac{C_0}{3} = C_0 \left[1 - \text{erf}\left(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{D \cdot 600}}\right)\right] \] Simplifying, we get: \[ \frac{1}{3} = 1 - \text{erf}\left(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{D \cdot 600}}\right) \] \[ \text{erf}\left(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{D \cdot 600}}\right) = \frac{2}{3} \] The inverse error function \(\text{erf}^{-1}\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)\) can be found using Python.

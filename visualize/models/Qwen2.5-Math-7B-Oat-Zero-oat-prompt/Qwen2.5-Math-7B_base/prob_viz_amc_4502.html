The final answer can be written in the form $\frac{m}{n}$, where $m$ and $n$ are relatively prime positive integers. What is $m+n$? Assistant:
To solve this problem, let's define the amount of orange juice in each glass. Let's assume each glass has a capacity of 1 unit of juice. Mrs. Jones fills the first three glasses completely, so each of these glasses has 1 unit of juice. The fourth glass is only \(\frac{1}{3}\) full, so it has \(\frac{1}{3}\) units of juice. The total amount of juice is: \[ 1 + 1 + 1 + \frac{1}{3} = 3 + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{10}{3} \text{ units of juice} \] If the juice is to be evenly distributed among the four glasses, each glass should have: \[ \frac{\frac{10}{3}}{4} = \frac{10}{12} = \frac{5}{6} \text{ units of juice} \] currently has \(\frac{1}{3}\) units of juice, so it needs: \[ \frac{5}{6} - \frac{1}{3} = \frac{5}{6} - \frac{2}{6} = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2} \text{ units of juice} \] Each of the first three glasses currently has 1 unit of juice, so each needs to give up: \[ 1 - \frac{5}{6} = \frac{6}{6} - \frac{5}{6} = \frac{1}{6} \text{ units of juice} \] Since the total amount of juice transferred from the first three glasses to the fourth glass must be \(\frac{1}{2}\) units, and this is the sum of the juice transferred from each of the first three glasses, the amount transferred from each of the first three glasses is: \[ \frac{\frac{1}{2}}{3} = \frac{1}{6} \text{ units of juice} \] So, the fraction of a glass that must be poured from each of the first three glasses into the fourth glass is \(\frac{1}{6}\). The fraction \(\frac{1}{6}\) is already in its simplest form, so \(m = 1\) and \(n = 6\). Therefore, \(m + n = 1 + 6 = 7\). The final answer is: \[ \boxed{7} \] </answer>

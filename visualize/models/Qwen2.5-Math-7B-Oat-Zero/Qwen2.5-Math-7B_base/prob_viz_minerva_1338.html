To solve this problem, we need to follow these steps: 1. **Calculate the energy of the photon**: The energy \(E\) of a photon is given by the equation \(E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}\), where \(h\) is Planck's constant, \(c\) is the speed of light, and \(\lambda\) is the wavelength of the photon. 2. **Determine the energy required to excite the electron**: The energy required to excite the electron from the ground state to the first excited state (n=2) in a hydrogen atom is given by the Rydberg formula for the energy difference between the two states. The energy of the electron in the ground state (n=1) is \(-13.6 \text{ eV}\) and in the first excited state (n=2) is \(-3.4 \text{ eV}\). So, the energy required to excite the electron is \(13.6 \text{ eV} - 3.4 \text{ eV} = 10.2 \text{ eV}\). 3. **Convert the energy from eV to Joules**: Since \(1 \text{ eV} = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}\), the energy required to excite the electron is \(10.2 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}\). 4. **Calculate the velocity of the electron**: The kinetic energy of the electron after absorbing the photon's energy is equal to the energy of the photon minus the energy required to excite the electron. The kinetic energy of the electron is given by \(\frac{1}{2}mv^2\), where \(m\) is the mass of the electron and \(v\) is the velocity of the electron. We can solve for \(v\) using the equation \(\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = E_{\text{photon}} - E_{\text{excitation}}\).

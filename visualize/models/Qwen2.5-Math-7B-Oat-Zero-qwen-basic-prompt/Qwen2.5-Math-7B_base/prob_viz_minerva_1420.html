In the short-term, a firm maximizes its profits by producing at the output level where marginal revenue equals marginal cost (MR = MC). Here's the step-by-step reasoning: 1. **Define the Profit Function:** The profit (\(\pi\)) of a firm is given by the difference between total revenue (TR) and total cost (TC): \[ \pi = TR - TC \] where \(TR = P \cdot Q\) (P is the price and Q is the quantity) and \(TC = TFC + TVC\) (TFC is total fixed cost and TVC is total variable cost). 2. **Maximize the Profit:** To maximize profit, we need to find the output level \(Q\) where the marginal profit is zero. The marginal profit is the derivative of the profit function with respect to \(Q\): \[ \frac{d\pi}{dQ} = \frac{d(TR)}{dQ} - \frac{d(TC)}{dQ} = MR - MC \] Setting the marginal profit to zero gives: \[ MR - MC = 0 \implies MR = MC \] This is the condition that describes a firm maximizing its profits in the short-term. So, the algebraic condition that describes a firm that is at an output level that maximizes its profits, given its capital in the short-term, is: \[ \boxed{MR = MC} \]

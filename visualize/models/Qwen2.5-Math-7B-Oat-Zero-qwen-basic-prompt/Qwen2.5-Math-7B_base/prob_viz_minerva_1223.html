The apparent magnitude \(m\) of a star is given by the formula: \[ m = -2.5 \log_{10} \left( \frac{F}{F_0} \right) \] where \(F\) is the flux (intensity) of the star as received by the observer, and \(F_0\) is a reference flux. The flux \(F\) of a star is proportional to its luminosity \(L\) divided by the square of its distance \(d\) from the observer: \[ F = \frac{L}{4 \pi d^2} \] The luminosity \(L\) of a star is proportional to its surface area and its surface temperature raised to the fourth power (Stefan-Boltzmann law): \[ L = 4 \pi R^2 \sigma T^4 \] where \(R\) is the radius of the star, \(\sigma\) is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant, and \(T\) is the surface temperature. Since the surface temperatures of the two stars are the same, the ratio of their luminosities is simply the ratio of their surface areas: \[ \frac{L_1}{L_2} = \frac{4 \pi R_1^2 \sigma T^4}{4 \pi R_2^2 \sigma T^4} = \left( \frac{R_1}{R_2} \right)^2 = (2.5)^2 = 6.25 \] Given that star 1 is ten times farther away than star 2, the ratio of their fluxes is: \[ \frac{F_1}{F_2} = \frac{L_1 / (4 \pi d_1^2)}{L_2 / (4 \pi d_2^2)} = \frac{L_1}{L_2} \cdot \left( \frac{d_2}{d_1} \right)^2 = 6.25 \cdot \left( \frac{1}{10} \right)^2 = 6.25 \cdot \frac{1}{100} = 0.0625 \] The apparent magnitude difference \(\Delta m\) between the two stars is given by: \[ \Delta m = m_1 - m_2 = -2.5 \log_{10} \left( \frac{F_1}{F_2} \right) = -2.5 \log_{10} (0.0625) \] We can now calculate this value using Python. ```python import math # Given ratio of fluxes flux_ratio = 0.0625 # Calculate the magnitude difference magnitude_difference = -2.5 * math.log10(flux_ratio) magnitude_difference_rounded = round(magnitude_difference) print(magnitude_difference_rounded) ``` ```output 4 ``` The absolute value of the difference in apparent magnitude between the two stars is \(\boxed{4}\).

To determine the rate constant \(k\) for a first-order chemical reaction, we can use the Arrhenius equation: \[ k = A \exp\left(-\frac{E_A}{RT}\right) \] where: - \(k\) is the rate constant, - \(A\) is the pre-exponential factor, - \(E_A\) is the activation energy, - \(R\) is the gas constant (8.314 J/(mol·K)), - \(T\) is the temperature in Kelvin. Given: - \(E_A = 250 \text{ kJ/mol} = 250000 \text{ J/mol}\), - \(A = 1.7 \times 10^{14} \text{ s}^{-1}\), - \(T = 750^\circ \text{C} = 750 + 273.15 = 1023.15 \text{ K}\). Let's substitute these values into the Arrhenius equation and calculate the rate constant \(k\). First, we need to calculate the exponent term: \[ -\frac{E_A}{RT} = -\frac{250000 \text{ J/mol}}{8.314 \text{ J/(mol·K)} \times 1023.15 \text{ K}} \] Then we can calculate the value of the exponential function and finally the rate constant \(k\).

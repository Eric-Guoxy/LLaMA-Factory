The absolute magnitude \(M\) of a star is related to its luminosity \(L\) by the formula: \[ M = M_{\odot} - 2.5 \log_{10} \left( \frac{L}{L_{\odot}} \right) \] where \(M_{\odot}\) is the absolute magnitude of the Sun and \(L_{\odot}\) is the luminosity of the Sun. Given that the absolute magnitude of the Sun \(M_{\odot} = +5\) and the luminosity of the Sun \(L_{\odot} = 3.83 \times 10^{33} \text{ ergs/s}\), we need to find the luminosity \(L\) of a star with an absolute magnitude \(M = 0\). Rearranging the formula to solve for \(L\): \[ 0 = 5 - 2.5 \log_{10} \left( \frac{L}{3.83 \times 10^{33}} \right) \] \[ 2.5 \log_{10} \left( \frac{L}{3.83 \times 10^{33}} \right) = 5 \] \[ \log_{10} \left( \frac{L}{3.83 \times 10^{33}} \right) = 2 \] \[ \frac{L}{3.83 \times 10^{33}} = 10^2 = 100 \] \[ L = 100 \times 3.83 \times 10^{33} = 3.83 \times 10^{35} \text{ ergs/s} \] Let's confirm this calculation using Python. ```python # Given values M_sun = 5 # absolute magnitude of the Sun L_sun = 3.83e33 # luminosity of the Sun in ergs/s M_star = 0 # absolute magnitude of the star # Calculate the luminosity of the star L_star = L_sun * (10**((M_sun - M_star) / 2.5)) print(L_star) ``` ```output 3.83e+35 ``` The luminosity of a star with an absolute magnitude of 0 is \(\boxed{3.83 \times 10^{35} \text{ ergs/s}}\).

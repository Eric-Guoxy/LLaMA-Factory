To solve this problem, we need to use the photoelectric effect equation. The photoelectric effect equation relates the energy of the incident photon to the work function of the metal and the kinetic energy of the emitted electron. The equation is given by: \[ E_{\text{photon}} = \text{Work Function} + \text{Kinetic Energy} \] The energy of the incident photon \(E_{\text{photon}}\) can be calculated using the formula: \[ E_{\text{photon}} = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \] where \(h\) is Planck's constant, \(c\) is the speed of light, and \(\lambda\) is the wavelength of the incident light. The work function \(\phi\) of chromium is given as \(4.40 \text{ eV}\). We need to convert this to Joules: \[ \phi = 4.40 \text{ eV} \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J/eV} = 7.0488 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} \] The wavelength \(\lambda\) is given as \(200 \text{ nm}\), which is \(200 \times 10^{-9} \text{ m} = 2 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}\). Now we can calculate the energy of the photon: \[ E_{\text{photon}} = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s} \times 3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}}{2 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}} = \frac{1.9878 \times 10^{-25} \text{ J m}}{2 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}} = 9.939 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} \] Now we can use the photoelectric effect equation to find the kinetic energy of the emitted electron: \[ E_{\text{photon}} = \phi + \text{Kinetic Energy} \] \[ 9.939 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} = 7.0488 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} + \text{Kinetic Energy} \] \[ \text{Kinetic Energy} = 9.939 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} - 7.0488 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} = 2.8902 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} \] So the kinetic energy of the emitted electrons is \(2.8902 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}\).
